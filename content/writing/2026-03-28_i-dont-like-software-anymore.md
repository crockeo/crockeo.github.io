# Rant

I don't like software anymore.

I don't know if I actually ever liked software, but I definitely don't now.

Yesterday I went onto [Door Dash](https://www.doordash.com/) and ordered Taco Bell.

- I don't like that they remember my email, but don't remember that I always log in with my password. I don't want to be texted a login code.

- I don't like that it takes 4 seconds to load the homepage, and another 5 seconds to load the Taco Bell page. My download speed is 173Mbps according to [Cloudflare](https://speed.cloudflare.com/). Why do we need to download 15MB of data to order food (and so much more uncompressed)?

- I don't like that they are harvesting and selling my data all the while.

- I don't like that clicking on the Crunchwrap Supreme takes 500ms to paint the dialog pane,and another 1.5s to add it to the cart.

This is not a takedown of DoorDash. DoorDash is not a particularly bad website. It is just one such website in a sea of slow, buggy software that we use every day. This just happpens to be my most recent experience.

I always imagine that this happens in a similar way:

- A small team makes a product. They are chiefly concerned with making something good, because having a good product sells. The product is small, and so everyone feels like their marginal contribution to product quality is important. Empowerment and financial incentives align towards the whole product being good.

- As a small team grows into a medium team and a medium-sized product, they teeter on the edge of misalignment. People begin to be isolated into niches. Their concept of success morphs into a local optimum. People feel less individually responsible for the quality of the product as a whole. The marginal impact one can have on the product diminishes. _But_ it is still possible for a driven executive 

- Once a team finally gets to be a large team, everything breaks down.

and so everyone can fit it entirely in their head. The financial incentives are aligned towards the whole product being good.

- The small team is successful. Their product finds users, the product grows, and the team grows. The product is at the outer reaches of what can fit in a single person's head. It is now easier to accidentally harm the user experience of the platform. The team is still small enough that you can fit everyone in a room. Whenever things slip too much, it is easy to reorient the team towards making a good product.




In posting this I worry that someone *from* DoorDash might read this post and feel personally offended, but I don't want that either. No one wakes up in the morning and thinks "today I am going to make a website that's annoying to use." These kinds of things happen because it is easy to build software like this. It is the natural course for software.

Imagine a product, any product. It started with a person or small team of people aligned on a vision. As that product becomes larger there are more and more product concerns. There are more engineering concerns. The small team becomes bigger and bigger and bigger until it is a big team.

The incentives for an individual on a small team are clear: make a good thing and people will want to use it. On a big team things are muddied. The highest leverage thing an individual can do is to be recognized for their work *within* their company. One's work is typically measured against the goals of their team. Those goals are typically metrics. One can do ✨ great work ✨, move the metric, be rewarded (financially, reputationally, whatever), and make the product worse. Say a medium/semi-large company has ~2,000-4,000 engineers. Multiply this dynamic thousands of times over.

This problem is also so tightly linked to capital. Companies work this way because it brings in more money. There is (almost) no money to be made in building something great. If you are giving someone a service, and no one else gives them that service better, they will just choose whoever.

But as a consumer it just sucks. I don't want to be annoyed using a computer every day. I don't want to be locked into using products like this.
